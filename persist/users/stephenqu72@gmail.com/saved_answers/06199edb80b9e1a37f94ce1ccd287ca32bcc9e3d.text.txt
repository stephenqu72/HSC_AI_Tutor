To find the expression for displacement $x$ in terms of velocity $v$, we use Newton's Second Law of Motion ($F = ma$).

### Step 1: Set up the equation of motion
The particle is moving horizontally and is opposed by a resistive force of $mkv + mkv^2$. Using $F = ma$, and noting that the force is resistive (acting in the opposite direction to velocity, hence negative):
$$ma = - (mkv + mkv^2)$$
Dividing both sides by $m$ (where $m > 0$):
$$a = - (kv + kv^2) = -k(v + v^2)$$

### Step 2: Express acceleration in terms of velocity and displacement
In rectilinear motion, acceleration can be expressed as $a = v \frac{dv}{dx}$. Substituting this into our equation:
$$v \frac{dv}{dx} = -k(v + v^2)$$

### Step 3: Rearrange to solve for $x$
We want an expression for $x$, so we separate the variables $x$ and $v$:
$$v \, dv = -k(v + v^2) \, dx$$
Rearranging to isolate $dx$:
$$dx = \frac{v}{-k(v + v^2)} \, dv$$
Simplify the right-hand side by canceling the $v$ term (assuming $v \neq 0$):
$$dx = -\frac{1}{k} \cdot \frac{v}{v(1 + v)} \, dv$$
$$dx = -\frac{1}{k} \cdot \frac{1}{1 + v} \, dv$$

### Step 4: Integrate to find $x$
To find $x$, we integrate both sides:
$$x = \int -\frac{1}{k} \frac{1}{1 + v} \, dv$$
$$x = -\frac{1}{k} \int \frac{1}{1 + v} \, dv$$

Comparing this result to the given options:
- A. $\frac{1}{k} \int \frac{1}{1+v} dv$
- B. $\frac{1}{k} \int \frac{1}{v+v^2} dv$
- C. $-\frac{1}{k} \int \frac{1}{v+v^2} dv$
- D. $-\frac{1}{k} \int \frac{1}{1+v} dv$

The derived expression matches option **D**.

### Final Answer
The correct expression is $x = -\frac{1}{k} \int \frac{1}{1+v} \, dv$.
