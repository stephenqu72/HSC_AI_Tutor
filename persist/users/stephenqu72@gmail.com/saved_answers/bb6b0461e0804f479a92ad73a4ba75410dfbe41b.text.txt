To address the statement "the sum of two irrational numbers is an irrational number," we must investigate whether this holds true for all pairs of irrational numbers.

### Part e
The statement is **false**. We can disprove the claim by providing a counter-example.

**Definition:** An irrational number is a real number that cannot be expressed as a fraction $\frac{p}{q}$ where $p$ and $q$ are integers and $q \neq 0$.

**Counter-example:**
Consider the two numbers $x = \sqrt{2}$ and $y = -\sqrt{2}$.

1.  **Justification of irrationality:**
    It is a standard result that $\sqrt{2}$ is irrational. Since $-\sqrt{2} = -1 \times \sqrt{2}$, and the product of a non-zero rational number and an irrational number is irrational, $-\sqrt{2}$ is also irrational.

2.  **Calculation of the sum:**
    Summing these two numbers, we get:
    $$x + y = \sqrt{2} + (-\sqrt{2}) = 0$$

3.  **Conclusion:**
    The result of the sum is $0$. Since $0$ can be expressed as the fraction $\frac{0}{1}$ (where $0$ and $1$ are integers and $1 \neq 0$), $0$ is a **rational number**.

Because the sum of these two irrational numbers results in a rational number, the original statement is proven false by counter-example.

**Final Answer:** The statement is false. A counter-example is $\sqrt{2} + (-\sqrt{2}) = 0$, which is rational.
