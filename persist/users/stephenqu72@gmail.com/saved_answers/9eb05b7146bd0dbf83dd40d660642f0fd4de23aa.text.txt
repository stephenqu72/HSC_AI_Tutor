To find a possible value for $z$ satisfying the equation $z^3 = \frac{i}{\bar{z}}$, we follow these steps:

### Worked Solution

**1. Algebraic manipulation**
Given the equation:
$$z^3 = \frac{i}{\bar{z}}$$
Assuming $z \neq 0$, we can multiply both sides by $\bar{z}$:
$$z^3 \bar{z} = i$$

**2. Expressing $z$ in polar form**
Let $z = re^{i\theta}$, where $r = |z| > 0$ and $\theta = \arg(z)$.
Recall that the conjugate is given by $\bar{z} = re^{-i\theta}$.
Substituting these into our equation:
$$(re^{i\theta})^3 (re^{-i\theta}) = i$$
$$r^3 e^{i3\theta} \cdot r e^{-i\theta} = i$$
$$r^4 e^{i2\theta} = i$$

**3. Expressing $i$ in polar form**
We know that $i = e^{i\frac{\pi}{2}}$. Thus, the equation becomes:
$$r^4 e^{i2\theta} = e^{i\frac{\pi}{2}}$$

**4. Solving for $r$ and $\theta$**
Equating the magnitudes and arguments:
*   **Magnitude:** $r^4 = 1$. Since $r$ is a real, positive radius, $r = 1$.
*   **Argument:** $2\theta = \frac{\pi}{2} + 2k\pi$ for any integer $k$.
    Dividing by 2:
    $$\theta = \frac{\pi}{4} + k\pi$$

For $k = 0$, $\theta = \frac{\pi}{4}$. This gives $z = e^{i\frac{\pi}{4}}$.
For $k = 1$, $\theta = \frac{5\pi}{4}$, which gives $z = e^{i\frac{5\pi}{4}}$.

**5. Comparing with options**
Looking at the provided options:
A. $e^{i\frac{\pi}{4}}$ matches our derived solution for $k=0$.
B. $e^{-i\frac{\pi}{4}}$ would result in $r^4 e^{-i\frac{\pi}{2}} = e^{-i\frac{\pi}{2}} \neq i$.
C. $e^{i\frac{\pi}{3}}$ would result in $e^{i\frac{2\pi}{3}} \neq i$.
D. $e^{-i\frac{\pi}{3}}$ would result in $e^{-i\frac{2\pi}{3}} \neq i$.

**Conclusion**
The value $z = e^{i\frac{\pi}{4}}$ satisfies the given equation.

Final Answer: **A**
