To identify a counterexample to a logical proposition of the form "If $P$, then $Q$", we must look for a scenario where the condition $P$ is satisfied, but the conclusion $Q$ is false.

### Analysis of the Proposition

The given proposition is: "If a positive integer is **not a perfect square** ($P$), then it is **not prime** ($Q$)."

In formal logic, this is the implication $P \implies Q$. 

A counterexample to this proposition must satisfy:
1. The antecedent ($P$): The integer is **not a perfect square**.
2. The negation of the consequent ($\neg Q$): The integer **is prime** (since the negation of "not prime" is "is prime").

### Evaluation of Options

*   **Option A:** "A positive integer that is not a perfect square and is prime."
    *   This satisfies both conditions ($P$ and $\neg Q$). For example, the number $3$ is not a perfect square and is prime. This directly contradicts the proposition.
*   **Option B:** "A positive integer that is not a perfect square and is not prime."
    *   This satisfies the proposition ($P \implies Q$), so it is not a counterexample.
*   **Option C:** "A positive integer that is a perfect square and is prime."
    *   There are no prime numbers that are perfect squares (a perfect square $n^2$ where $n > 1$ has at least three factors: $1, n, n^2$), so this does not satisfy the antecedent $P$.
*   **Option D:** "A positive integer that is a perfect square and is not prime."
    *   This satisfies the proposition (the proposition says nothing about perfect squares), so it is not a counterexample.

### Conclusion
A counterexample must demonstrate that it is possible for a number to be "not a perfect square" while still being "prime". Option A describes exactly this condition.

Final Answer: A
