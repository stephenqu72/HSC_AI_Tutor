To find the value of the integral $I = \int_{0}^{\pi} x \sin x \, dx$ using the substitution $x = \pi - y$, we perform the following steps:

### Worked Solution

1.  **Define the substitution:**
    Given $x = \pi - y$, we find the differential $dx$ by differentiating with respect to $y$:
    $$\frac{dx}{dy} = -1 \implies dx = -dy$$

2.  **Change the limits of integration:**
    *   When $x = 0$, then $0 = \pi - y$, which gives $y = \pi$.
    *   When $x = \pi$, then $\pi = \pi - y$, which gives $y = 0$.

3.  **Substitute into the integral:**
    Substitute $x$, $dx$, and the new limits into the integral expression:
    $$I = \int_{\pi}^{0} (\pi - y) \sin(\pi - y) (-dy)$$

4.  **Simplify the expression:**
    *   Use the trigonometric identity $\sin(\pi - y) = \sin y$.
    *   Use the negative sign from $-dy$ to swap the order of the limits (Property of definite integrals: $-\int_{a}^{b} f(y) \, dy = \int_{b}^{a} f(y) \, dy$):
    $$I = \int_{0}^{\pi} (\pi - y) \sin y \, dy$$

5.  **Expand and split the integral:**
    $$I = \int_{0}^{\pi} \pi \sin y \, dy - \int_{0}^{\pi} y \sin y \, dy$$
    Note that the second term $\int_{0}^{\pi} y \sin y \, dy$ is equivalent to the original integral $I$ (as the choice of variable does not change the definite integral value). Therefore:
    $$I = \pi \int_{0}^{\pi} \sin y \, dy - I$$
    $$2I = \pi \int_{0}^{\pi} \sin y \, dy$$
    $$I = \frac{\pi}{2} \int_{0}^{\pi} \sin y \, dy$$
