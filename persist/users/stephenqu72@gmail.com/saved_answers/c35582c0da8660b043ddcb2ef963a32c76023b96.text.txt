To find the negation of a conditional statement, we must use the principles of formal logic.

### Logical Analysis

1.  **Define the propositions:**
    Let $P$ be the statement "Snow is falling".
    Let $Q$ be the statement "I will be at the park".
    The original statement is "If snow is falling, then I will not be at the park".
    Symbolically, this is written as: $P \implies \neg Q$ (If $P$, then not $Q$).

2.  **Recall the rule for negation:**
    The negation of a conditional statement $P \implies R$ is $P \land \neg R$ (P and not R).
    In this case, our "result" part of the implication is $\neg Q$. Therefore, the negation of the entire implication is:
    $$P \land \neg(\neg Q)$$

3.  **Simplify the expression:**
    By the Law of Double Negation, $\neg(\neg Q)$ is equivalent to $Q$.
    Thus, the logical form of the negation is:
    $$P \land Q$$
    In plain English, this translates to: "Snow is falling AND I will be at the park."

4.  **Evaluate the options:**
    *   A) If $P$, then $Q$. (This is a different conditional statement).
    *   B) If $\neg P$, then $Q$. (This is a different conditional statement).
    *   C) Snow is falling and I will be at the park. (Matches our derived form $P \land Q$).
    *   D) $\neg P \land Q$. (This is not the negation of the original statement).

The negation of the statement is: **Snow is falling and I will be at the park.**

This corresponds to option **C**.
