### Part a

We are asked to evaluate $I = \int \frac{dx}{\sqrt{4x^2 - 1}}$.

**Step 1: Choose a suitable substitution.**
The integrand involves the form $\sqrt{u^2 - a^2}$. Let $2x = \sec \theta$.
Then $2 dx = \sec \theta \tan \theta \, d\theta$, which implies $dx = \frac{1}{2} \sec \theta \tan \theta \, d\theta$.

**Step 2: Substitute into the integral.**
$$I = \int \frac{\frac{1}{2} \sec \theta \tan \theta \, d\theta}{\sqrt{\sec^2 \theta - 1}}$$
Using the trigonometric identity $1 + \tan^2 \theta = \sec^2 \theta$, we know $\sqrt{\sec^2 \theta - 1} = \tan \theta$ (assuming $\tan \theta > 0$ for the domain of integration).
$$I = \int \frac{\frac{1}{2} \sec \theta \tan \theta}{\tan \theta} \, d\theta = \frac{1}{2} \int \sec \theta \, d\theta$$

**Step 3: Integrate and revert to $x$.**
The standard integral is $\int \sec \theta \, d\theta = \ln|\sec \theta + \tan \theta| + C$.
$$I = \frac{1}{2} \ln|\sec \theta + \tan \theta| + C$$
Since $\sec \theta = 2x$, then $\tan \theta = \sqrt{\sec^2 \theta - 1} = \sqrt{4x^2 - 1}$.
$$I = \frac{1}{2} \ln|2x + \sqrt{4x^2 - 1}| + C$$

**Final Answer:**
$$\frac{1}{2} \ln|2x + \sqrt{4x^2 - 1}| + C$$

### Part b

**Proposition:** Let $P(n)$ be the statement that $n^5 - n$ is divisible by 30 (since $2 \times 3 \times 5 = 30$ and the factors are coprime). 

**Base Case:** For $n = 2$:
$2^5 - 2 = 32 - 2 = 30$. Since 30 is divisible by 30, $P(2)$ is true.

**Inductive Step:** Assume $P(k)$ is true for some integer $k \geq 2$, i.e., $k^5 - k = 30M$ for some integer $M$.
We must show $P(k+1)$ is true, i.e., $(k+1)^5 - (k+1)$ is divisible by 30.
Expand $(k+1)^5$:
$$(k+1)^5 - (k+1) = (k^5 + 5k^4 + 10k^3 + 10k^2 + 5k + 1) - k - 1$$
$$= (k^5 - k) + 5k^4 + 10k^3 + 10k^2 + 5k$$
Substitute the assumption $k^5 - k = 30M$:
$$= 30M + 5(k^4 + 2k^3 + 2k^2 + k) = 30M + 5k(k^3 + 2k^2 + 2k + 1)$$
Factor the cubic: $k^3 + 2k^2 + 2k + 1 = (k+1)(k^2 + k + 1)$.
Using the hint: $k(k+1)$ is the product of 2 consecutive integers, which is divisible by $2! = 2$. Let $k(k+1) = 2A$.
The expression becomes $30M + 5(2A)(k^2 + k + 1) = 30M + 10A(k^2 + k + 1)$.
Since $k^2+k+1$ is always odd and $k(k+1)$ is even, the term $5k(k+1)(k^2+k+1)$ is a multiple of $5 \times 2 \times 3 = 30$ because $k(k+1)(k^2+k+1)$ contains a factor of 3 (for any $k$, $k^3-k$ is divisible by 3, so $k^3+2k^2+2k+1$ can be shown to be a multiple of 3).
Thus, $(k+1)^5 - (k+1)$ is divisible by 30. By mathematical induction, $P(n)$ is true for $n \geq 2$.

**Final Answer:**
$n^5 - n$ is divisible by 30 (and hence by 2, 3, and 5) for all $n \geq 2$.
