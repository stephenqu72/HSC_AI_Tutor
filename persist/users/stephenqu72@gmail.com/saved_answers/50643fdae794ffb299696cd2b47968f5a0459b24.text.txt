To solve these problems involving complex numbers, we will use the modulus-argument form $w = r(\cos \theta + i \sin \theta)$, often written as $re^{i\theta}$, and De Moivre's Theorem.

### Part i
**Task:** Express $w = -\sqrt{3} + i$ in modulus-argument form.

1. **Calculate the modulus $r$:**
   The modulus $r = |w| = \sqrt{x^2 + y^2}$, where $x = -\sqrt{3}$ and $y = 1$.
   $$r = \sqrt{(-\sqrt{3})^2 + (1)^2} = \sqrt{3 + 1} = \sqrt{4} = 2$$

2. **Calculate the argument $\theta$:**
   The complex number $w = -\sqrt{3} + i$ lies in the second quadrant. 
   The reference angle $\alpha$ satisfies $\tan \alpha = \left| \frac{1}{-\sqrt{3}} \right| = \frac{1}{\sqrt{3}}$.
   Thus, $\alpha = \frac{\pi}{6}$.
   Since the number is in the second quadrant, $\theta = \pi - \frac{\pi}{6} = \frac{5\pi}{6}$.

3. **Conclusion:**
   Substituting $r=2$ and $\theta = \frac{5\pi}{6}$ into $r(\cos \theta + i \sin \theta)$:
   $$w = 2\left(\cos \frac{5\pi}{6} + i \sin \frac{5\pi}{6}\right)$$

Final Answer: $w = 2\left(\cos \frac{5\pi}{6} + i \sin \frac{5\pi}{6}\right)$

### Part ii
**Task:** Hence or otherwise show that $w^7 + 64w = 0$.

1. **Use De Moivre's Theorem:**
   From part (i), $w = 2 e^{i \frac{5\pi}{6}}$. Therefore, $w^7 = (2 e^{i \frac{5\pi}{6}})^7$.
   Using the index law $(ab)^n = a^n b^n$:
   $$w^7 = 2^7 \cdot e^{i \frac{35\pi}{6}} = 128 \left(\cos \frac{35\pi}{6} + i \sin \frac{35\pi}{6}\right)$$

2. **Simplify the argument:**
   We note that $\frac{35\pi}{6} = \frac{36\pi - \pi}{6} = 6\pi - \frac{\pi}{6}$.
   Since $6\pi$ is a multiple of $2\pi$, the trigonometric values are equivalent to those at $-\frac{\pi}{6}$:
   $$w^7 = 128 \left(\cos(-\frac{\pi}{6}) + i \sin(-\frac{\pi}{6})\right)$$
   Since $\cos(-\theta) = \cos \theta$ and $\sin(-\theta) = -\sin \theta$:
   $$w^7 = 128 \left(\cos \frac{\pi}{6} - i \sin \frac{\pi}{6}\right) = 128 \left(\frac{\sqrt{3}}{2} - \frac{1}{2}i\right) = 64\sqrt{3} - 64i$$

3. **Evaluate $w^7 + 64w$:**
   We know $w = -\sqrt{3} + i$.
   $$64w = 64(-\sqrt{3} + i) = -64\sqrt{3} + 64i$$
   Adding the two expressions:
   $$w^7 + 64w = (64\sqrt{3} - 64i) + (-64\sqrt{3} + 64i)$$
   $$w^7 + 64w = 0$$

Final Answer: Since $w^7 = 64\sqrt{3} - 64i$ and $64w = -64\sqrt{3} + 64i$, their sum is 0.
