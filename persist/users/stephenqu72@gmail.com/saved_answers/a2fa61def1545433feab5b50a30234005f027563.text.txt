To solve the integral $I = \int_{0}^{\pi} x \sin x \, dx$ using the substitution $x = \pi - u$, we follow these rigorous steps:

### Worked Solution

**1. Define the substitution and limits:**
Let $x = \pi - u$.
Then, differentiate with respect to $u$: $\frac{dx}{du} = -1$, which implies $dx = -du$.

Next, change the limits of integration:
*   When $x = 0$: $0 = \pi - u \implies u = \pi$.
*   When $x = \pi$: $\pi = \pi - u \implies u = 0$.

**2. Substitute into the integral:**
Substitute $x$, $dx$, and the new limits into the original integral $I$:
$$I = \int_{\pi}^{0} (\pi - u) \sin(\pi - u) (-du)$$

**3. Simplify the expression:**
*   Use the property of definite integrals $\int_{a}^{b} f(u) (-du) = \int_{b}^{a} f(u) du$ to absorb the negative sign by swapping the limits back:
    $$I = \int_{0}^{\pi} (\pi - u) \sin(\pi - u) \, du$$
*   Apply the trigonometric identity $\sin(\pi - u) = \sin u$:
    $$I = \int_{0}^{\pi} (\pi - u) \sin u \, du$$
*   Expand the integrand:
    $$I = \int_{0}^{\pi} \pi \sin u \, du - \int_{0}^{\pi} u \sin u \, du$$
    $$I = \pi \int_{0}^{\pi} \sin u \, du - I$$
*   Add $I$ to both sides:
    $$2I = \pi \int_{0}^{\pi} \sin u \, du$$
*   Divide by 2:
    $$I = \frac{\pi}{2} \int_{0}^{\pi} \sin u \, du$$

**4. Final form:**
Since the variable of integration is a "dummy variable", we can replace $u$ with $x$ without changing the value of the definite integral:
$$I = \frac{\pi}{2} \int_{0}^{\pi} \sin x \, dx$$
