To evaluate the integral $I = \int \frac{\ln x}{(1+\ln x)^2} dx$, we will use the method of substitution to simplify the integrand.

### Step 1: Perform the substitution
Let $u = \ln x$.
Then, by the definition of the derivative of the natural logarithm, we have:
$$\frac{du}{dx} = \frac{1}{x} \implies du = \frac{1}{x} dx$$
From $u = \ln x$, we can exponentiate both sides to get $x = e^u$. Therefore, $dx = x \, du = e^u \, du$.

### Step 2: Substitute into the integral
Substituting $u$ and $dx$ into the original integral:
$$I = \int \frac{u}{(1+u)^2} \cdot e^u \, du$$
To make this expression easier to integrate, we perform a clever algebraic manipulation on the numerator by adding and subtracting 1:
$$I = \int e^u \left( \frac{u + 1 - 1}{(1+u)^2} \right) du$$
$$I = \int e^u \left( \frac{u + 1}{(1+u)^2} - \frac{1}{(1+u)^2} \right) du$$
$$I = \int e^u \left( \frac{1}{1+u} - \frac{1}{(1+u)^2} \right) du$$

### Step 3: Recognise the standard form
We now recognise this in the form $\int e^u [f(u) + f'(u)] \, du$, where $f(u) = \frac{1}{1+u}$.
The derivative is:
$$f'(u) = \frac{d}{du}(1+u)^{-1} = -(1+u)^{-2} \cdot 1 = -\frac{1}{(1+u)^2}$$
Since the integrand is exactly $e^u [f(u) + f'(u)]$, the integral is $e^u f(u) + C$:
$$I = e^u \left( \frac{1}{1+u} \right) + C$$

### Step 4: Back-substitute
Substitute $u = \ln x$ back into the expression:
$$I = e^{\ln x} \left( \frac{1}{1 + \ln x} \right) + C$$
Since $e^{\ln x} = x$, we get:
$$I = \frac{x}{1 + \ln x} + C$$

**Final Answer:**
$$\int \frac{\ln x}{(1+\ln x)^2} dx = \frac{x}{1 + \ln x} + C$$
