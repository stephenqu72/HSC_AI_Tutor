Remember that in motion problems, defining your coordinate system is the first crucial step. We will define the downward direction as positive, so $x$ is the distance fallen, $v = \dot{x}$ is the downward velocity, and $\ddot{x}$ is the downward acceleration.

---

### Part i
**Goal:** Show that $\ddot{x} = g - \frac{v^2}{100}$.

**Derivation:**
According to Newton's Second Law, the net force acting on the body is the sum of the gravitational force acting downwards and the resistance force acting upwards (opposing motion).
The body has unit mass ($m = 1$).
- Gravitational force = $mg = (1)(g) = g$.
- Resistance force = $\frac{v^2}{100}$.

Net force $F = ma = m\ddot{x}$.
$$m\ddot{x} = mg - R$$
$$(1)\ddot{x} = g - \frac{v^2}{100}$$
$$\ddot{x} = g - \frac{v^2}{100}$$

**Final Answer:**
$\ddot{x} = g - \frac{v^2}{100}$

---

### Part ii
**Goal:** Determine the terminal velocity $V$.

**Method:**
Terminal velocity occurs when the acceleration is zero ($\ddot{x} = 0$). As the body falls, the resistance increases until it equals the gravitational force, resulting in no further change in velocity.
Setting the equation from part (i) to zero:
$$0 = g - \frac{V^2}{100}$$
$$\frac{V^2}{100} = g$$
$$V^2 = 100g$$
$$V = \sqrt{100g} = 10\sqrt{g}$$

**Final Answer:**
$V = 10\sqrt{g} \text{ m/s}$

---

### Part iii
**Goal:** Show $v^2 = V^2\left(1 - e^{-\frac{x}{50}}\right)$.

**Derivation:**
We use the alternative expression for acceleration: $\ddot{x} = v \frac{dv}{dx}$.
Substituting this into the equation from part (i):
$$v \frac{dv}{dx} = g - \frac{v^2}{100}$$
Since $V^2 = 100g$, then $g = \frac{V^2}{100}$. Substituting this:
$$v \frac{dv}{dx} = \frac{V^2}{100} - \frac{v^2}{100} = \frac{1}{100}(V^2 - v^2)$$
Separate the variables $v$ and $x$:
$$\frac{v}{V^2 - v^2} \, dv = \frac{1}{100} \, dx$$
Integrate both sides. To integrate the left side, use substitution $u = V^2 - v^2$, so $du = -2v \, dv$:
$$-\frac{1}{2} \int \frac{1}{u} \, du = \int \frac{1}{100} \, dx$$
$$-\frac{1}{2} \ln|V^2 - v^2| = \frac{x}{100} + C$$
At $x=0$, $v=0$. Thus, $-\frac{1}{2} \ln|V^2| = C$.
$$-\frac{1}{2} \ln(V^2 - v^2) = \frac{x}{100} - \frac{1}{2} \ln(V^2)$$
$$\frac{1}{2} \ln(V^2) - \frac{1}{2} \ln(V^2 - v^2) = \frac{x}{100}$$
$$\ln\left(\frac{V^2}{V^2 - v^2}\right) = \frac{2x}{100} = \frac{x}{50}$$
Exponentiate both sides:
$$\frac{V^2}{V^2 - v^2} = e^{\frac{x}{50}}$$
$$V^2 = (V^2 - v^2)e^{\frac{x}{50}} \implies V^2e^{-\frac{x}{50}} = V^2 - v^2$$
$$v^2 = V^2 - V^2e^{-\frac{x}{50}} = V^2\left(1 - e^{-\frac{x}{50}}\right)$$

**Final Answer:**
$v^2 = V^2\left(1 - e^{-\frac{x}{50}}\right)$

---

### Part iv
**Goal:** Determine the distance $x$ when $v = \frac{V}{2}$.

**Method:**
Substitute $v = \frac{V}{2}$ into the formula derived in part (iii):
$$\left(\frac{V}{2}\right)^2 = V^2\left(1 - e^{-\frac{x}{50}}\right)$$
$$\frac{V^2}{4} = V^2\left(1 - e^{-\frac{x}{50}}\right)$$
Divide by $V^2$ (as $V \neq 0$):
$$\frac{1}{4} = 1 - e^{-\frac{x}{50}}$$
$$e^{-\frac{x}{50}} = 1 - \frac{1}{4} = \frac{3}{4}$$
Take the natural logarithm of both sides:
$$-\frac{x}{50} = \ln\left(\frac{3}{4}\right)$$
$$x = -50 \ln\left(\frac{3}{4}\right) = 50 \ln\left(\frac{4}{3}\right)$$

**Final Answer:**
$x = 50 \ln\left(\frac{4}{3}\right) \text{ metres}$
