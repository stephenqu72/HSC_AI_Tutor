To solve the integral $\int \frac{1+x}{4+x^2} dx$, we observe that the integrand can be split into two separate fractions to simplify the integration process using standard integral forms.

### Step-by-step Solution

**1. Decomposition of the Integral**
We can rewrite the integrand as:
$$\int \frac{1+x}{4+x^2} dx = \int \frac{1}{4+x^2} dx + \int \frac{x}{4+x^2} dx$$

**2. Evaluating the first integral**
The first part, $\int \frac{1}{4+x^2} dx$, is in the standard form $\int \frac{1}{a^2+x^2} dx = \frac{1}{a} \tan^{-1}\left(\frac{x}{a}\right) + C$.
Here, $a^2 = 4$, so $a = 2$.
$$\int \frac{1}{2^2+x^2} dx = \frac{1}{2} \tan^{-1}\left(\frac{x}{2}\right)$$

**3. Evaluating the second integral**
For the second part, $\int \frac{x}{4+x^2} dx$, we use the method of substitution.
Let $u = 4+x^2$. Then $du = 2x \, dx$, which implies $\frac{1}{2} du = x \, dx$.
$$\int \frac{x}{4+x^2} dx = \int \frac{1}{u} \cdot \frac{1}{2} du = \frac{1}{2} \ln|u|$$
Substituting $u = 4+x^2$ back into the expression:
$$\frac{1}{2} \ln(4+x^2)$$
*(Note: we drop the absolute value bars because $4+x^2$ is always positive for all real $x$)*.

**4. Combining the results**
Adding the two parts together and including the constant of integration $C$:
$$\int \frac{1+x}{4+x^2} dx = \frac{1}{2} \tan^{-1}\left(\frac{x}{2}\right) + \frac{1}{2} \ln(4+x^2) + C$$

**Final Answer:**
$$\frac{1}{2} \tan^{-1}\left(\frac{x}{2}\right) + \frac{1}{2} \ln(4+x^2) + C$$
