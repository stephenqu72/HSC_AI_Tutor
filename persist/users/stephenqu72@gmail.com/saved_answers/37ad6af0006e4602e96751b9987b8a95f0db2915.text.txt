To prove the statement $x^2 - 4y \neq 2$ for all integers $x, y \in \mathbb{Z}$, we will use a proof by contradiction.

### Proof

1.  **Assume the negation of the proposition:**
    Assume there exist integers $x$ and $y$ such that:
    $$x^2 - 4y = 2$$

2.  **Rearrange the equation:**
    We can isolate the $x^2$ term:
    $$x^2 = 4y + 2$$
    Factoring the right side:
    $$x^2 = 2(2y + 1)$$

3.  **Analyze the parity of $x$:**
    Since $2y + 1$ is an integer (because $y \in \mathbb{Z}$), the expression $2(2y + 1)$ is clearly even. Therefore, $x^2$ must be an even integer. 
    If the square of an integer is even, the integer itself must also be even. Thus, let $x = 2k$ for some integer $k \in \mathbb{Z}$.

4.  **Substitute and simplify:**
    Substitute $x = 2k$ into our rearranged equation:
    $$(2k)^2 = 2(2y + 1)$$
    $$4k^2 = 2(2y + 1)$$
    Divide both sides by 2:
    $$2k^2 = 2y + 1$$

5.  **Examine parity for contradiction:**
    The left side of the equation, $2k^2$, is clearly even.
    The right side of the equation, $2y + 1$, is by definition an odd integer for any integer $y$.
    This leads to the statement:
    $$\text{Even} = \text{Odd}$$
    This is a contradiction, as no integer can be both even and odd simultaneously.

6.  **Conclusion:**
    Since our initial assumption that there exist integers $x, y$ such that $x^2 - 4y = 2$ leads to a contradiction, the assumption must be false. Therefore, for all $x, y \in \mathbb{Z}$, $x^2 - 4y \neq 2$.
