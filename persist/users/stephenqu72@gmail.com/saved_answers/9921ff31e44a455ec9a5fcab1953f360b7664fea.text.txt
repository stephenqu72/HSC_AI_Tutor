To prove that $n^3 - n$ is divisible by 6 for all integers $n$, we can use algebraic factorisation and the properties of consecutive integers.

### Part c
**Goal:** Prove that $n^3 - n$ is divisible by 6 for all $n \in \mathbb{Z}$.

**Step 1: Factorise the expression.**
We begin by factorising the expression $n^3 - n$:
$$n^3 - n = n(n^2 - 1)$$
Using the difference of two squares identity, $a^2 - b^2 = (a-b)(a+b)$, we can factorise $(n^2 - 1)$:
$$n^3 - n = n(n - 1)(n + 1)$$
Rearranging the factors in ascending order, we have:
$$n^3 - n = (n - 1)n(n + 1)$$

**Step 2: Justification using properties of consecutive integers.**
The expression $(n - 1)n(n + 1)$ represents the product of three consecutive integers.
1.  **Divisibility by 2:** In any two consecutive integers, at least one must be even (divisible by 2). Therefore, the product of three consecutive integers must be divisible by 2.
2.  **Divisibility by 3:** In any three consecutive integers, exactly one must be a multiple of 3. Therefore, the product of three consecutive integers must be divisible by 3.

**Step 3: Conclusion.**
Since the expression is divisible by both 2 and 3, and because 2 and 3 are coprime (i.e., their greatest common divisor is 1), the expression must be divisible by their product:
$$2 \times 3 = 6$$
Thus, $(n-1)n(n+1)$ is divisible by 6 for all integers $n$.

**Final Answer:**
Since $n^3 - n = (n-1)n(n+1)$ is the product of three consecutive integers, it contains at least one multiple of 2 and one multiple of 3. Therefore, it is divisible by $2 \times 3 = 6$.
